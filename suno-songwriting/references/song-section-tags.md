# Song Section Tags

Core lyric-section tags for ordinary song structure.

## Core structure
- [Intro] — Opens the piece and establishes sonic context before the main body arrives. Expected result: gives the listener orientation and sets tone, palette, and expectation. Consider using when the genre benefits from scene-setting, signature timbre, or a strong first atmosphere.

- [Opening Motif] — Introduces a short recurring musical idea that can unify the composition. Expected result: creates thematic coherence and makes later returns feel intentional. Consider using when you want a memorable seed that can recur across sections.

- [Verse] — Main narrative or idea-development section, usually with evolving lyrics over a stable harmonic/rhythmic frame. Expected result: advances story, perspective, or detail without exhausting the central hook. Consider using when the song needs exposition, scene-building, or lyrical progression.

- [Pre-Chorus] — Transitional lift between verse and chorus that increases expectation. Expected result: heightens tension and makes the chorus land harder. Consider using when the chorus needs stronger setup or the verse-to-chorus jump feels too abrupt.

- [Chorus] — Primary payoff section with the strongest melodic and lyrical hook. Expected result: delivers memorability, emotional release, and structural anchor. Consider using when you want the core message or catchiest material to recur clearly.

- [Hook] — The most instantly memorable phrase, riff, or rhythmic idea, whether inside or outside the chorus. Expected result: increases recall value and commercial stickiness. Consider using when a section needs a clear takeaway or repeatable signature.

- [Post-Chorus] — A follow-up passage after the chorus that extends momentum with chant, riff, or slogan-like material. Expected result: keeps energy elevated and deepens memorability without repeating the whole chorus. Consider using when the chorus lands well but feels too short or needs extra propulsion.

- [Refrain] — A repeated line or musical idea that returns across sections, not necessarily as a full chorus. Expected result: gives the song a recurring anchor and emotional thread. Consider using when you want repetition but not a full pop-chorus architecture.

- [Bridge] — Contrasting section that breaks the pattern established by verse/chorus material. Expected result: refreshes the listener’s ear and adds emotional or harmonic perspective before the final return. Consider using when the form needs contrast, escalation, or a narrative turn.

- [Breakdown] — Reduces arrangement density or groove complexity to spotlight rhythm, voice, or tension. Expected result: creates contrast, prepares a rebuild, or intensifies physical impact. Consider using when a genre favors drops, mosh moments, chant sections, or dancefloor resets.

- [Interlude] — Short transitional passage, often instrumental or textural, between major sections. Expected result: adds breathing room, atmosphere, or narrative pacing. Consider using when the track needs a palette cleanser or cinematic connective tissue.

- [Instrumental] — A section where instruments take over primary expression from the vocal. Expected result: showcases timbre, groove, or virtuosity while widening the emotional space. Consider using when the genre values solos, ensemble interplay, or scenic expansion.

- [Outro] — Closing section that releases, resolves, or reframes the track’s energy. Expected result: gives the song a satisfying exit rather than an abrupt stop. Consider using when the ending should feel deliberate, immersive, or emotionally legible.

- [Coda] — A distinct final add-on after the apparent ending, often offering one last statement. Expected result: creates a more composed, “written” finish and can deepen thematic closure. Consider using when the ending deserves an epilogue rather than a simple repeat-and-stop.

- [Cold Ending] — Stops decisively on a final hit or cadence with no fade. Expected result: feels confident, emphatic, and final. Consider using when you want strong punctuation, dramatic authority, or live-band clarity.

- [Fade Out] — Gradually decreases level while the musical material continues. Expected result: implies continuity beyond the recording and leaves a lingering aftertaste. Consider using when groove or vamp is the point and a hard ending would feel too artificial.
