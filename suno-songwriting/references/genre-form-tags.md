# Genre Form Tags

Genre-specific movement tags for roots, jazz, EDM, pop, rock, heavy music, rap, Latin, Afro-diasporic, devotional, Asian chamber, gamelan, and social-space forms.

## Grand formal and orchestral gestures
- [Grand Overture] — Opens with a large-scale, declarative introduction that announces the world of the piece. Expected result: prestige, scope, and immediate dramatic framing. Consider using when the music aims for symphonic, theatrical, cinematic, or ceremonial impact.

- [Triple-Meter Lift] — Emphasizes the buoyancy, sweep, or rotational feel of three-beat motion. Expected result: elegance, dance arc, or swirling propulsion. Consider using in waltz-derived passages, folk turns, ballroom allusions, or hybrid sections needing circular flow.

## Roots, folk, and vernacular markers
- [Front-Porch Intro] — Suggests an intimate, vernacular, close-mic, human-scale opening. Expected result: warmth, authenticity, and lived-in storytelling. Consider using for blues, country, singer-songwriter, folk, and acoustic roots hybrids.

- [Turnaround Lick] — Uses a compact phrase to cycle back toward the next verse or formal loop. Expected result: continuity, groove punctuation, and idiomatic roots credibility. Consider using in blues, jazz, country, zydeco, rockabilly-adjacent, or riff-based songwriting.

## Jazz process and improvisation markers
- [Head In] — States the main tune before improvisation or development begins. Expected result: formal clarity and a shared reference point for later departures. Consider using in jazz-derived works, fusion, or instrumental pieces with improvisational sections.

- [Trading Fours] — Alternates short improvised phrases between players or sections, often in four-bar units. Expected result: lively dialogue, virtuosity, and ensemble playfulness. Consider using when the track benefits from conversational improvisation or a live-session feel.

## Process and minimalist motion
- [Loop Expansion] — A repeating cell grows in length, density, or harmonic implication over time. Expected result: hypnotic momentum with gradual evolution rather than abrupt change. Consider using in minimalism, techno, post-rock, trance, ambient pop, or slow-build cinematic structures.

- [Process Reveal] — Lets the listener perceive the underlying compositional mechanism as it unfolds. Expected result: elegance, inevitability, and intellectual satisfaction. Consider using when repetition and transformation are central, and when form itself is part of the expressive content.

## EDM and drop architecture
- [DJ Intro] — Starts in a mix-friendly, beat-oriented, or atmosphere-led way suitable for blending into a set. Expected result: functional entry plus scene-setting without immediate full payoff. Consider using in club genres, extended mixes, or tracks that need modular opening space.

- [Riser Build] — Uses upward motion, filtered noise, snare ramps, or harmonic lift to signal an incoming peak. Expected result: obvious anticipation and sharpened release impact. Consider using before drops, choruses, beat arrivals, or major transitions.

- [Main Drop] — The principal beat-and-bass release point after tension buildup. Expected result: maximum dancefloor payoff and identity statement. Consider using when rhythmic impact is the core event of the track.

- [Second Drop] — A later drop that either intensifies or mutates the first. Expected result: renewed excitement and avoidance of formulaic repetition. Consider using when the track needs a second major payoff with variation.

- [Fake Drop] — Teases the drop, then withholds or redirects it. Expected result: surprise, crowd manipulation, and heightened eventual release. Consider using when tension-management is part of the fun or when you want to subvert expectation.

- [Euphoric Breakdown] — Strips the beat while foregrounding emotional harmony, pads, or topline. Expected result: uplift, scale, and pre-drop emotional expansion. Consider using in trance, festival pop, future bass, melodic house, or cinematic EDM hybrids.

- [Wobble Assault] — Brings in aggressive modulated bass as the dominant hook element. Expected result: tactile impact, sonic spectacle, and maximal bass-character identity. Consider using in dubstep, neurofunk hybrids, heavy bass music, or deliberately abrasive drops.

## Commercial pop architecture
- [Hook First] — Places the catchiest idea at or near the beginning instead of waiting for a later payoff. Expected result: immediate engagement and strong retention from the first seconds. Consider using in mainstream pop, short-form attention economies, or highly replay-driven songs.

- [Big Chorus] — Marks a chorus designed for scale, lift, and direct memorability. Expected result: strong emotional payoff and broad accessibility. Consider using when the song’s center of gravity should be obvious and large.

- [Second Verse Switch-Up] — Keeps the second verse from feeling like a copy through rhythmic, lyrical, or arrangement variation. Expected result: freshness and forward motion without sacrificing form. Consider using when the first verse material is strong but exact repetition would flatten momentum.

- [Rap Insert] — Adds a rhythm-forward vocal subsection with contrasting cadence and density. Expected result: textural variety, character contrast, and broadened genre vocabulary. Consider using in pop hybrids, K-pop, Afrobeats-pop, R&B-pop, or club-friendly structures.

- [Ad-Lib Finale] — Ends with increasingly free vocal embellishment over repeated core material. Expected result: emotional overflow and a live-feeling climax. Consider using for final choruses, gospel-inflected peaks, diva-pop endings, or soulful closures.

## Band-entry and rock-form markers
- [Guitar Count-In] — Signals a band-led entry with immediacy and live-energy implication. Expected result: rawness, momentum, and a grounded human start. Consider using in rock, punk, indie, garage, or roots-inflected tracks.

- [Full Band Entry] — The entire ensemble arrives together after a partial or sparse opening. Expected result: dramatic expansion and stronger physical impact. Consider using when you want the section to feel like the song fully “starts” there.

- [Wall-of-Guitars] — Stacks multiple guitar layers into a dense, high-energy texture. Expected result: width, force, and emotional saturation. Consider using in alternative rock, shoegaze-leaning pop, power ballad climaxes, post-rock peaks, or anthem choruses.

- [Singalong Refrain] — Shapes a repeated line for collective participation over lyrical complexity. Expected result: high crowd recall and communal energy. Consider using in pop punk, arena rock, folk-rock, festival choruses, or any section meant to be shouted back.

## Heavy music markers
- [Double-Kick Launch] — Introduces rapid bass drum drive as an energy-defining gesture. Expected result: instant intensity, propulsion, and metallic authority. Consider using in power metal, symphonic metal, metalcore, or fast dramatic entrances.

- [Breakdown] — Collapses motion into a heavier, more impact-focused rhythmic section. Expected result: bodily force, tension concentration, and often crowd-response behavior. Consider using in metalcore, hardcore-adjacent forms, trap-metal hybrids, or aggressive drop moments.

- [Symphonic Choir Layer] — Adds choral/orchestral mass over heavy or grand material. Expected result: epic scale, ceremonial drama, and harmonic grandeur. Consider using when metal, cinematic, or fantasy-coded material needs a larger-than-band dimension.

## Post-rock and slow-burn cinematic markers
- [Ambient Opening] — Begins with spacious, low-attack texture rather than immediate groove or hook. Expected result: immersion and a wide emotional horizon. Consider using when mood, environment, or slow revelation matters more than instant structural clarity.

- [Patient Build] — Makes escalation deliberate and cumulative rather than obvious or quick. Expected result: deep payoff and emotional credibility in the eventual crest. Consider using in post-rock, cinematic indie, ambient-rock hybrids, or long-form instrumentals.

- [Cloudburst] — A sudden arrival of full emotional and textural force after restraint. Expected result: overwhelming catharsis and dramatic contrast. Consider using for a delayed climax, distorted bloom, orchestral release, or final explosive arrival.

## Rap production and beat-form markers
- [Beat Switch] — Changes the beat significantly mid-track in rhythm, timbre, or mood. Expected result: surprise, sectional distinction, and expanded dramatic range. Consider using when a single beat cannot carry the full emotional arc or when flex/contrast is desirable.

- [808 Entry] — Marks the moment the sub-bass becomes a primary expressive force. Expected result: increased weight, menace, or bodily pull. Consider using in trap, drill, hybrid pop-rap, or any arrangement where low-end arrival should feel significant.

- [Hi-Hat Run] — Features a burst of rapid hat subdivision as a momentum accent. Expected result: heightened urgency, modern rhythmic identity, and textural sparkle. Consider using before transitions, in flex moments, or to sharpen trap-derived energy.

- [Producer Tag Intro] — Opens or punctuates with a maker-signature cue associated with beat culture. Expected result: instant scene context and stylistic legitimacy. Consider using in rap, trap, drill, club hybrids, or production-forward forms where authorship branding is part of the feel.

## Club and Latin urban markers
- [Dembow Entry] — Brings in the signature dembow-derived rhythmic engine. Expected result: immediate dance identity and bodily recognition. Consider using in reggaetón, dembow, Latin club hybrids, or any crossover section needing unmistakable rhythmic coding.

- [Perreo Drop] — Creates a low, physical, body-led release oriented toward reggaetón dance energy. Expected result: sensual impact and floor-level groove payoff. Consider using when the track needs a heavier, more kinetic club turn.

- [Kick-Triplet Burst] — Uses triplet kick articulation as a high-energy punctuation device. Expected result: urgency, bounce, and battle-ready club tension. Consider using in Jersey club, hybrid dance forms, or transitional hype moments.

## Afro-diasporic groove and vamp logic
- [Groove Lock] — Commits fully to a cyclical rhythm pocket as the section’s identity. Expected result: trance, bodily immersion, and confidence. Consider using in Afrobeat, Afrobeats, funk, highlife, mbaqanga, amapiano, kwaito, and any groove-first framework.

- [Extended Vamp] — Repeats a harmonic/rhythmic cell long enough for micro-variation and accumulation to matter. Expected result: deep pocket, ensemble conversation, and collective lift over time. Consider using when groove development is more important than rapid chord change.

- [Horn Call] — Uses brass or reed lines as declarative rallying figures. Expected result: authority, celebration, and public-space energy. Consider using in Afrobeat, samba-enredo, highlife, parade music, funk, or large-ensemble pop hybrids.

- [Log Drum Reveal] — Introduces the signature percussive bass identity associated with amapiano-like motion. Expected result: immediate stylistic recognition and buoyant low-end character. Consider using in amapiano, Afro-house crossovers, or hybrid club sections wanting that rolling bass personality.

## Clave, Iberian, and Latin ensemble procedures
- [Clave Set] — Makes the clave relationship explicit in the groove architecture. Expected result: stronger Afro-Cuban grounding and more coherent rhythmic placement across parts. Consider using in son-derived writing, salsa-adjacent forms, Latin jazz, or clave-conscious hybrids.

- [Montuno Launch] — Begins the vamping, cyclical call-and-response engine associated with son-derived forms. Expected result: increased momentum, participation, and dance-forward repetition. Consider using when shifting from song statement into groove expansion or jam logic.

- [Coro/Pregón] — Splits the vocal field into group refrain and lead exclamation. Expected result: social dynamism, stronger repetition, and idiomatic Latin ensemble feel. Consider using in son, salsa-adjacent hybrids, carnival forms, or communal dance structures.

- [Palmas Cycle] — Uses handclaps as a rhythmic scaffold and expressive intensifier. Expected result: embodied pulse, flamenco/ritual association, and heightened tension. Consider using when hand rhythm itself should carry identity, especially in flamenco, folk, or stripped rhythmic passages.

- [Bandoneon Lament] — Signals a sharply expressive, sighing, dramatic melodic turn associated with tango affect. Expected result: emotional sophistication, ache, and urban-night drama. Consider using in tango-inflected sections, cinematic melancholy, or sharply contoured instrumental lines.

- [Saudade Pause] — Creates a reflective suspension that foregrounds longing and inward emotional space. Expected result: intimate poignancy and dignified ache. Consider using in fado-like moments, lyrical ballad pivots, or endings that need wistful gravity.

## South Asian devotional and classical markers
- [Alap] — Opens with unmetered melodic exploration around the tonal center and mood-world. Expected result: deep atmosphere, seriousness, and patient emotional grounding. Consider using before pulse enters, especially in raga-influenced, devotional, or meditative structures.

- [Drone Invocation] — Begins over a sustained tonal field that frames the mode and spiritual center. Expected result: gravity, focus, and ritual readiness. Consider using when tonal rootedness, contemplation, or ceremonial framing matters.

- [Tala Arrival] — Marks the transition from free melodic space into regulated cyclical rhythm. Expected result: satisfying structural arrival and stronger bodily anchoring. Consider using when you want the listener to feel the beat as a meaningful event, not a default assumption.

- [Devotional Peak] — Frames the climax as intensifying surrender, praise, yearning, or spiritual union. Expected result: catharsis with reverence rather than purely secular triumph. Consider using in qawwali-like, bhajan-informed, or sacred-hybrid passages.

## East Asian chamber, ritual, and spatial markers
- [Ma Space] — Intentionally foregrounds charged silence or intervallic spaciousness as part of the expression. Expected result: heightened attention, delicacy, and temporal depth. Consider using when restraint, breath, and contemplative pacing are more important than continuous fill.

- [Measured Silence] — Inserts silence with deliberate structural timing rather than as accidental absence. Expected result: drama, ceremony, and sharpened perception of what follows. Consider using in ritual forms, theater music, sparse chamber writing, or transition points requiring gravity.

## Gamelan and interlocking ceremonial markers
- [Gong Cycle Opening] — Establishes a cyclical formal frame with gong punctuation or its conceptual equivalent. Expected result: ceremonial structure and strong temporal orientation. Consider using in gamelan-inspired writing, ritualized percussion forms, or circular ensemble architecture.

- [Kotekan Entry] — Introduces rapid interlocking figuration split across parts. Expected result: shimmer, virtuosity, and explosive collective motion. Consider using when precision interdependence and flashing surface energy are desired.

- [Flash Ending] — Concludes suddenly with bright, decisive, high-energy punctuation. Expected result: excitement, virtuosity, and a clean ceremonial snap. Consider using when a long fade would weaken the style’s taut brilliance.

## Communal and vernacular social-space markers
- [Communal Circle Entry] — Frames the section as participatory, shared, and socially embodied rather than solo-presentational. Expected result: warmth, invitation, and collective ownership of the groove. Consider using in folk, son jarocho, village-dance, chant, and community-centered traditions.

- [Wedding Peak] — Signals a celebratory apex built for dance, festivity, and shared joy. Expected result: extroversion, uplift, and unmistakable communal climax. Consider using in klezmer-like, folk-dance, brass-led, or festive crossover arrangements.

- [Tearful Rubato] — Uses expressive timing elasticity to heighten sorrow, yearning, or intimate pathos. Expected result: vulnerability and emotionally detailed phrasing. Consider using in lament traditions, sevdalinka-like moments, fado, art-ballads, or fragile solo passages.
