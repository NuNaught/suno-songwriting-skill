# Instrument Knowledge

Use instrumentation to define arrangement, texture, genre, and motion. Pick a focused palette and give each important sound a job. A strong Suno instrument direction is usually role-first, not list-first: "fingerpicked guitar carries the pulse" is stronger than "guitar."

## Core Workflow

1. Identify the arrangement anchor: rhythm, harmony, riff/hook, texture, or cinematic color.
2. Choose a compact palette for ordinary prompts, or a deliberately unusual stack for fusion experiments.
3. Assign roles: pulse, groove, harmonic bed, counterline, hook, drop impact, response, lift, glue, or outro color.
4. Shape density by section: sparse verse, rising pre-chorus, widened chorus, stripped bridge, rebuild, final chorus.
5. Add production behavior: dry room, tape warmth, glossy compression, wide stereo pads, sidechain pump, live-room bleed, saturated edge.

## Role Vocabulary

| Role | Prompt verbs | Useful when |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pulse carrier | carries, ticks, drives, chops, strums, arpeggiates | The song needs clear motion without clutter. |
| Groove anchor | locks, leans, swings, pushes, lays back | Rhythm feel matters more than instrumentation novelty. |
| Harmonic bed | cushions, sustains, blooms, drones, warms | The vocal needs support and emotional space. |
| Signature hook | answers, riffs, doubles, sparkles, punctuates | The song needs a memorable non-vocal identity. |
| Counterline | weaves, replies, shadows, circles, descends | Choruses or bridges need motion around the vocal. |
| Impact layer | hits, slams, swells, drops, explodes | Cinematic, rock, trap, EDM, or theatrical builds. |
| Texture glue | washes, smears, saturates, shimmers, breathes | Atmosphere or cohesion matters more than notes. |
| Contrast color | strips down, exposes, thins, suspends | Bridge, breakdown, intro, or outro needs contrast. |

## Rhythm Section

- **Live drums**: tight backbeat, brushed drums, roomy kit, gated snare, half-time groove, double kick, tom-heavy cinematic build.
- **Programmed drums**: boom-bap drums, trap hats, drill slides, four-on-the-floor kick, garage swing, breakbeat chop, glitch percussion.
- **Bass**: electric bass, upright bass, sub bass, 808 bass, Reese bass, synth bass, walking bass, muted picked bass, octave disco bass.
- **Percussion**: handclaps, tambourine, shaker, congas, bongos, clave, rim clicks, taiko, frame drum, cinematic toms.

Use rhythm terms with feel: straight, swung, behind-the-beat, ahead-of-beat, syncopated, half-time, double-time, motorik, shuffle, clave-aware, pocketed.

## Harmony Instruments

- **Guitar**: acoustic strum, fingerpicked acoustic, clean electric, jangly guitar, palm-muted power chords, distorted riff, shoegaze wall, slide guitar, pedal steel.
- **Keys**: piano, felt piano, Rhodes, Wurlitzer, Hammond organ, clavinet, synth pads, plucks, arpeggiators, bell keys.
- **Orchestral/chamber**: string quartet, cello counterline, pizzicato strings, brass swells, woodwind color, harp gliss, timpani, choir.
- **Folk/global color**: banjo, mandolin, fiddle, accordion, oud, qanun, kora, sitar, sarangi, erhu, morin khuur, mbira, balafon, dulcimer, hurdy-gurdy, nyckelharpa, duduk, shakuhachi, tin whistle, pan flute, hand percussion, drones.

Use tradition-specific instruments when they support the genre, setting, story world, or sonic experiment. For broad fusion, name the musical function as well as the instrument so unlikely combinations can still feel intentional.

## Unlikely Fusion Builder

For adventurous prompts, combine one anchor, one contrast, one bridge, and one production binder:

- **Anchor**: the main groove or genre engine, such as trap drums, synth-pop pulse, folk strum, metal riff, reggaeton dembow, or jazz swing.
- **Contrast**: the surprising instrument or tradition color, such as kora, shakuhachi, guembri, nyckelharpa, gamelan metallophones, or bandoneon.
- **Bridge**: a shared musical mechanism: drone, ostinato, call-and-response, modal melody, hand percussion, plucked arpeggio, or bowed counterline.
- **Production binder**: tape warmth, club sub, cinematic reverb, lo-fi sampling, granular processing, close-room realism, or glossy pop compression.

Useful fusion verbs: braid, collide, answer, shadow, pulse under, shimmer over, distort into, sample-chop, sidechain, double, trade phrases, bloom behind.

## Lead and Texture

- **Leads**: lead synth, sax solo, fiddle line, mandolin hook, distorted guitar riff, clarinet line, brass stab, whistle motif, glockenspiel sparkle.
- **Electronic texture**: ambient pads, sidechained pads, granular shimmer, reversed piano, vocal chops, glitch stutters, risers, impacts, filtered noise.
- **Organic texture**: room tone, tape hiss, finger noise, bow scrape, hand percussion, body percussion, close-mic breath, live-room bleed.
- **Cinematic texture**: low drones, bowed bass, taiko hits, brass swells, string ostinato, choir accents, sub drops, trailer impacts.

## Section Motion

- **Intro**: establish signature timbre, motif, drone, groove seed, or room tone.
- **Verse**: keep space for lyrics; use one pulse carrier and one harmonic bed.
- **Pre-chorus**: add lift through rising bass, opened hats, string swell, arpeggiator, or harmonic widening.
- **Chorus**: widen the palette; add stronger drums, doubled hook, counterline, harmony stack, or impact layer.
- **Bridge**: create contrast by stripping to voice plus one color, changing register, or suspending the groove.
- **Breakdown/drop**: reduce and rebuild, or switch focus to bass, drums, chant, riff, or texture.
- **Final chorus**: add cumulative lift: extra harmony, upper counterline, wider drums, brighter hook, or modulation support.
- **Outro**: resolve with the signature sound thinned down, delayed, or reharmonized.

## Genre Palette Starters

- **Singer-songwriter / folk**: fingerpicked guitar carries pulse, warm upright or soft electric bass, brushed drums, subtle fiddle/cello answers, dry close-room production.
- **Country / Americana**: acoustic guitar bed, pedal steel answers, steady bass, restrained kit, fiddle or mandolin hook, warm live-room realism.
- **Pop rock / alternative**: tight drums, driving bass, clean-to-distorted guitar layers, piano or synth pad support, big chorus doubles.
- **R&B / neo-soul**: Rhodes or Wurlitzer bed, pocketed drums, warm bass, muted guitar chops, stacked backing vocals, close-mic polish.
- **Hip-hop / trap**: 808 anchors chorus, crisp hats, sparse dark piano or synth loop, sub drops, filtered texture, tight vocal space.
- **EDM / dance-pop**: four-on-the-floor kick, sidechained synth bass, bright pluck hook, riser builds, drop impact, wide glossy mix.
- **Jazz / lounge**: piano or Rhodes comping, upright bass, brushed drums, horn or guitar fills, intimate room, behind-the-beat swing.
- **Cinematic / orchestral pop**: piano motif, string ostinato, brass swells, cinematic toms, choir accents, wide reverb and staged dynamics.
- **Ambient / experimental**: drones, processed piano, granular pads, sparse percussion, low sub movement, evolving texture instead of busy parts.
- **Global / folk fusion**: tradition-specific instruments with clear roles, grounded rhythm, familiar harmonic support, adventurous cross-palette color, and production glue.
- **Unlikely hybrid**: trap drums under kora arpeggios, taiko impacts answering synth drops, nyckelharpa drones behind industrial bass, gamelan patterns inside hyperpop sidechain.

## Guardrails

- Avoid listing every instrument in a band unless the arrangement needs that density, or unless the goal is an intentionally maximal fusion stack.
- Pair instruments with role, section, or production behavior.
- Keep the vocal clear: avoid busy lead instruments under dense lyrics.
- For fusion, stacking unrelated palettes is allowed when roles are clear: define what carries pulse, what leads, what drones, what answers, and what binds the mix.
- Do not put instrument names in the lyrics unless the user made instruments part of the song's story.
- Use section tags for instrumental breaks only when the lyric field needs them; otherwise put arrangement motion in the style prompt.
