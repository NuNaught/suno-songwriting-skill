| # | Voice type | Level / context | Description | Identifying characteristics |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Soprano** | Standard choral / classical | Highest common treble voice category. | High tessitura, bright or ringing upper register, often carries melody in choral writing. |
| 2 | **Alto** | Standard choral | Lower treble choral part, often sung by contraltos, mezzo-sopranos, or lower sopranos. | Lower tessitura than soprano, inner harmony role, often warm or blended tone. |
| 3 | **Tenor** | Standard choral / classical | Highest common non-treble adult voice category. | High male/non-treble tessitura, ringing upper register, often melodic or heroic line. |
| 4 | **Bass** | Standard choral / classical | Lowest standard choral voice category. | Low tessitura, foundational harmonic role, resonant lower register. |
| 5 | **Mezzo-soprano** | Expanded classical category | Middle treble category between soprano and contralto. | Richer/darker than many sopranos, strong middle register, often sings trouser roles or dramatic female roles. |
| 6 | **Contralto** | Expanded classical category | Lowest classical treble voice type; rarer than mezzo-soprano. | Dark, weighty lower and middle register, low tessitura, less soprano-like brilliance on top. |
| 7 | **Countertenor** | Expanded classical category | High adult non-treble voice, often using reinforced falsetto/head-voice-dominant production. | Treble range, clear or reedy timbre, common in early music and some modern opera. |
| 8 | **Baritone** | Expanded classical category | Middle non-treble voice between tenor and bass. | Strong middle register, lower than tenor but more upper extension than bass, often flexible dramatic casting. |
| 9 | **Bass-baritone** | Expanded classical / opera | Hybrid category between bass and baritone; not merely “halfway” in range. | Bass-like weight with strong upper access, often villain, authority, or Wagner/Mozart roles. |
| 10 | **Coloratura soprano** | Fach | Agile high soprano specializing in ornate, fast, florid music. | Very high extension, rapid runs, trills, leaps, lightness, brilliance. |
| 11 | **Lyric soprano** | Fach | Warm, melodic soprano suited to lyrical roles. | Smooth legato, clear upper register, moderate vocal weight, expressive line rather than sheer power. |
| 12 | **Dramatic soprano** | Fach | Large, powerful soprano for heavy orchestration and intense dramatic roles. | Strong projection, stamina, full tone, powerful high notes, often darker timbre. |
| 13 | **Spinto soprano** | Fach | “Pushed” or lyric-dramatic soprano between lyric and dramatic. | Lyric beauty plus bursts of power, can cut through fuller orchestration without being as heavy as dramatic soprano. |
| 14 | **Soubrette soprano** | Fach | Light soprano often cast in youthful, comic, maid, or ingénue roles. | Bright tone, light weight, excellent diction, agility, charm, moderate range demands. |
| 15 | **Lyric mezzo-soprano** | Fach | Lighter mezzo category suited to lyrical lines and trouser roles. | Warm middle register, flexibility, comfortable upper-middle tessitura, less weight than dramatic mezzo. |
| 16 | **Dramatic mezzo-soprano** | Fach | Powerful mezzo for intense, often mature or commanding roles. | Dark color, strong low and middle registers, stamina, dramatic thrust. |
| 17 | **Coloratura mezzo-soprano** | Fach | Agile mezzo capable of elaborate ornamentation. | Fast runs, flexibility, strong lower-middle register, ability to move through florid passages cleanly. |
| 18 | **Lyric tenor** | Fach | Lighter tenor suited to melodic, romantic, or youthful roles. | Clear upper register, sweetness, legato, less heft than spinto or dramatic tenor. |
| 19 | **Dramatic tenor / Heldentenor** | Fach | Heavy, powerful tenor for heroic or high-intensity roles, especially Wagnerian repertoire. | Large sound, stamina, strong upper register, darker timbre, ability to ride heavy orchestration. |
| 20 | **Basso profundo** | Fach / bass subtype | Exceptionally low bass voice, especially associated with Russian choral and operatic traditions. | Very low extension, deep resonance, dark timbre, strong low tessitura, “subterranean” bass quality. |

## Suggested hierarchy

**Vocal classification**  
→ **Choral voice category**: soprano, alto, tenor, bass  
→ **Expanded classical voice type**: soprano, mezzo-soprano, contralto, countertenor, tenor, baritone, bass-baritone, bass  
→ **Fach / subtype**: lyric soprano, dramatic mezzo, Heldentenor, basso profundo, and so on.


| Term | Definition | Why it matters for voice classification |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocal range** | The full span of pitches a singer can produce, from lowest to highest. | Useful, but not enough by itself to determine voice type. |
| **Tessitura** | The pitch area where a singer can sing most comfortably and sustainably. | Often more important than total range when identifying a voice type. |
| **Timbre** | The tone color or quality of a voice: bright, dark, warm, metallic, reedy, rich, etc. | Helps distinguish, for example, a mezzo-soprano from a soprano with similar notes. |
| **Vocal weight** | The perceived heaviness, lightness, size, or density of the sound. | Helps separate lyric, spinto, and dramatic categories. |
| **Vocal color** | Another term closely related to timbre; the character or “shade” of the sound. | Often used informally to describe brightness, darkness, warmth, or edge. |
| **Passaggio** | A transition area between vocal registers where coordination changes are needed. | Passaggi locations are important clues in classical voice classification. |
| **Register** | A region of the voice produced with a particular vocal coordination, such as chest voice, head voice, or falsetto. | Different voice types manage registers differently across their ranges. |
| **Chest voice** | A lower register or vocal coordination associated with a fuller, speech-like resonance. | Important for strength in lower and middle ranges. |
| **Head voice** | A higher register or coordination associated with lighter production and upper resonance. | Important for treble voices and upper extension. |
| **Falsetto** | A lighter, often breathier high-register production, especially discussed in lower-voiced singers. | Central to many countertenor techniques, though not identical to all countertenor singing. |
| **Modal voice** | The ordinary speaking/singing register used for much classical and contemporary singing. | The main register used to evaluate most adult voice types. |
| **Mix / mixed voice** | A coordinated blend of chest- and head-dominant qualities. | Helps singers move through register transitions smoothly. |
| **Treble** | A high voice or high pitch range; often used for sopranos, altos, children’s voices, and high instruments. | Useful as a broad category covering upper voices. |
| **Non-treble voice** | A lower adult voice category, often used instead of “male voice” when classification is based on range/function rather than gender. | Useful for inclusive terminology covering tenor, baritone, bass, etc. |
| **SATB** | Abbreviation for **soprano, alto, tenor, bass**, the standard four-part choral layout. | The core Western choral classification system. |
| **Part** | A musical line assigned to a singer or section, such as alto part or tenor part. | A choral part is not always the same as a singer’s true voice type. |
| **Section** | A group of singers assigned to the same part, such as the soprano section. | Choral section labels are practical ensemble roles, not always precise voice classifications. |
| **Fach** | A German-derived system for classifying operatic voices by range, tessitura, timbre, weight, agility, and repertoire. | Provides finer distinctions than basic choral categories. |
| **Subtype** | A narrower classification within a broader voice type, such as lyric, dramatic, or coloratura. | Helps describe more specific vocal capabilities. |
| **Lyric** | A lighter, smoother vocal quality suited to melodic, expressive singing. | Used in Fach labels to indicate warmth, flexibility, and moderate weight. |
| **Dramatic** | A heavier, more powerful vocal quality suited to intense roles and large orchestration. | Indicates size, stamina, projection, and vocal force. |
| **Spinto** | Literally “pushed”; a voice between lyric and dramatic that can sing lyrically but also produce powerful climaxes. | Useful for voices with both beauty and added thrust. |
| **Coloratura** | Highly ornamented, agile singing involving fast runs, leaps, trills, and embellishments. | Identifies voices especially suited to florid repertoire. |
| **Legato** | Smooth, connected singing from note to note. | A key technical and stylistic quality, especially in classical singing. |
| **Staccato** | Detached, separated notes. | Shows agility, articulation, and stylistic control. |
| **Agility** | The ability to sing fast passages, ornaments, leaps, and changes cleanly. | Important for coloratura and lighter Fach categories. |
| **Projection** | A voice’s ability to carry clearly across a hall or over accompaniment. | Important in opera and large choral/orchestral settings. |
| **Resonance** | The amplification and shaping of sound by the vocal tract. | Affects timbre, carrying power, and perceived richness. |
| **Formant** | A concentration of acoustic energy at certain frequencies shaped by resonance. | Helps explain why some voices carry strongly over instruments. |
| **Singer’s formant** | A resonance cluster, especially in classical singing, that helps the voice project over an orchestra. | Important for operatic projection. |
| **Vibrato** | A regular, slight oscillation in pitch and intensity during sustained singing. | Often considered part of mature classical tone when controlled and stylistically appropriate. |
| **Straight tone** | Singing with little or no vibrato. | Common in some choral, early music, and contemporary styles. |
| **Onset / attack** | The way a sung note begins. | Reveals coordination, breath control, and stylistic approach. |
| **Glottal onset** | A firm note beginning created by closure at the vocal folds. | Can be stylistically useful but may sound hard if overused. |
| **Aspirate onset** | A breathy note beginning with audible air before tone. | Can sound gentle, but overuse may suggest inefficient closure. |
| **Balanced onset** | A coordinated note beginning where breath and vocal fold closure align cleanly. | Often preferred in classical technique. |
| **Breath support** | The coordinated management of breath pressure and airflow while singing. | Essential for stability, stamina, dynamics, and tone. |
| **Appoggio** | A classical breath-management concept involving balanced support, expansion, and controlled release. | Central in many classical singing traditions. |
| **Dynamics** | Variations in loudness, such as soft and loud singing. | Helps evaluate control, expressivity, and repertoire suitability. |
| **Piano** | Soft singing. | Indicates control when singing quietly. |
| **Forte** | Loud singing. | Indicates power and projection. |
| **Messa di voce** | A controlled crescendo and decrescendo on a single sustained pitch. | Demonstrates refined breath control, resonance, and dynamic command. |
| **Leggiero / leggiero quality** | Light, flexible vocal quality. | Often used for agile, lighter roles or subtypes. |
| **Sustained singing** | Holding tones or phrases over time with stability. | Important for stamina and classical line. |
| **Stamina** | The ability to sing demanding music for extended periods without fatigue or loss of quality. | Crucial for dramatic and operatic roles. |
| **Extension** | The outer notes beyond a singer’s most comfortable core range. | Helps distinguish usable range from occasional notes. |
| **Core range** | The central, reliable part of a singer’s range. | More useful than extremes for classification. |
| **Upper extension** | A singer’s usable high notes beyond the main tessitura. | Important for soprano, tenor, and coloratura classifications. |
| **Lower extension** | A singer’s usable low notes beyond the main tessitura. | Important for contralto, bass, and basso profundo classifications. |
| **Repertoire** | The body of music or roles a singer performs. | Voice types are often defined partly by what repertoire fits the voice. |
| **Role suitability** | How well a singer’s voice fits the demands of a specific operatic or musical role. | Central to Fach classification. |
| **Orchestration** | The way instrumental parts are written around or under the singer. | Heavier orchestration requires more vocal carrying power. |
| **Choral blend** | The ability of voices to combine into a unified ensemble sound. | Important in choirs, where individual voice type may be less emphasized. |
| **Soloistic voice** | A voice quality suited to standing out as an individual line. | Important in opera, art song, and solo repertoire. |
| **Range notation** | The written indication of pitches a voice can sing, often using note names like C4 or A5. | Provides a technical way to describe range, though not full voice type. |
| **Middle C / C4** | The C near the center of the piano, commonly called middle C. | A reference point for describing vocal ranges. |
| **Octave designation** | A system for naming pitch height, such as C3, C4, C5. | Avoids ambiguity when discussing ranges. |
