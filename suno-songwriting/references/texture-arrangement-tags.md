# Texture and Arrangement Tags

Texture, ensemble, harmony, groove-layer, and sonic-treatment tags.

## Texture and ensemble behavior
- [Drone Bed] — Sustains one or a few anchor tones underneath the music. Expected result: creates gravity, ritual atmosphere, modal stability, or meditative depth. Consider using in chant, folk, raga-like, ambient, or tension-heavy settings.

- [Pedal Tone] — Holds a stable note, usually in the bass, while the harmony or upper material shifts above it. Expected result: adds tension, continuity, and architectural strength. Consider using to intensify transitions, climaxes, or formal development.

- [Sparse Percussion] — Uses minimal rhythmic support rather than a full groove. Expected result: leaves room for voice, harmony, or suspense while still implying motion. Consider using when a full beat would crowd the emotional space or arrive too early.

- [Full Rhythm Section] — Brings in the complete groove engine: drums, bass, and key rhythmic support. Expected result: fuller body, stronger pulse, and increased momentum. Consider using when you want a section to feel “arrived” or expanded.

- [Call and Response] — Alternates a lead phrase with an answering phrase from voice or instrument. Expected result: communal energy, rhetorical shape, and strong memorability. Consider using in traditions rooted in dialogue, participation, or ensemble interplay.

- [Layered Harmonies] — Stacks supporting vocal or instrumental pitches around the main line. Expected result: richer emotional color and bigger perceived scale. Consider using when a section needs warmth, grandeur, or heightened beauty.

- [Interlocking Pattern] — Splits a composite groove or melody across multiple parts. Expected result: intricate motion, momentum, and collective intelligence in the arrangement. Consider using in gamelan-like textures, funk, Afro-diasporic grooves, minimalist processes, or tight ensemble music.

- [Polyrhythmic Layer] — Adds simultaneous contrasting rhythmic grids or accents. Expected result: deeper groove complexity and more bodily engagement. Consider using when the genre supports layered time-feel, ritual intensity, or dance complexity.

- [Ostinato Bed] — Places repeating rhythmic or melodic material underneath a section. Expected result: stable hypnosis that supports development above. Consider using when you want continuity, trance, or pressure without constant harmonic change.

- [Filtered Texture] — Narrows the frequency spectrum or timbral brightness for a section. Expected result: creates anticipation, contrast, or a sense of distance before reopening the mix. Consider using for EDM builds, dreamlike transitions, or intro/outro framing.

- [Noise Wash] — Uses broadband texture, distortion, air, or environmental sound as an expressive layer. Expected result: expands atmosphere and can blur section boundaries in a cinematic way. Consider using in post-rock, dark electronic, industrial, ambient, or dramatic transitions.

- [Glitch Layer] — Adds digital interruption, stutter, error-like micro-events, or fragmented texture. Expected result: makes the section feel unstable, futuristic, playful, or hyper-detailed. Consider using in hyperpop, IDM-leaning pop, experimental electronic, or tension passages.
